I like tinkering with new ideas, even if similar solutions in the space exist because I believe other nascent ideas can grow from the fun of tinkering with something and feeling it. There's nothing more beautiful in tech than being able to do this at such a low cost. Other industries don't have this luxury that we have, and we should celebrate it, more so when we've got LLMs pushing the idea exploration cost down to zero.

With time I learned that this is often in tension with perfectionism and fear. Perfectionism might lead to someone never reaching a destination due to paralysis. There'll never be a perfect enough prototype to share with the world, leading to continuous working in vacuum towards something far away, and eventually giving up or procrastinating due to the long distance. This can easily kill companies. Fear does its part too. What if I can't bring anything meaningful? What if no one uses it? What if people criticise it cause I've gone my way? What if I prove the idea wasn't great to begin with? These are all the ingredients to companies and economies that lack innovation, stagnate, and potentially die, cause they are killed by their own way of operating sleeping on their dominant market positions.

For Marek and I, it became clear we don't want that to be the case at Tuist. First and foremost, exploring creative solutions to problems is what brings us the most fun, even if some of our prototypes end up being abandoned, so what? We have a record of pushing the right ideas to valuable solutions that people pay for. And this is something we quite liked from Shopify. The company always strived to explore the different shapes e-commerce could take and morph into, which at times meant building things that would go nowhere. So what?

I can't stop thinking a lot lately about how we should operate as a company where most of our operations are automated following our own take for how those processes should run. This always fascinated me about Shopify. They first came up with their working frameworks, and then built internal tools that reflected that, not the other way around. That method is now accessible to anyone that's willing to have a conversation with oneself regarding how they'd like to work. Most of our lead lifecycle management is now in our Atlas platform. I started moving all our finances there too, and I'm playing with a few frameworks to map infrastructure alerts and errors to a ready-to-review PR, and we'll keep adding more and more from there. And best of all is that while we have investors, we haven't sold any promise on hires to achieve X which I'm promising you, so we are optimizing for how to do more with less, bringing the talent where we think it's needed in this framework.

This expands beyond operations. We built an extra review partner, [Blick](https://github.com/tuist/blick) to bring extra review eyes and give us a foundation to iterate on the model, and I recently fell in love with the ideas behind swamp.club as a framework under harnesses where operations can be codified and shared across environments. I'm also tinkering with a sort of a build system or automation layer, drawing design decisions from Bazel, but designing it assuming agents would be the main elements interacting with it, and therefore a lot of design decisions should be done differently. As mentioned earlier, for some of these ideas, I don't know if they'll go anywhere. Many times after I've built something and felt it, or after I've played with the idea in my head for some time I come to the realization that it makes no sense, but we'd rather have Tuist remembered as a company that tried things and pushed the line for what's possible, than a company that became comfortable for what it was once, and that never had the appetite to go beyond that.

Coding agents brought us the foundation to build the company we want to build with such a small team.
